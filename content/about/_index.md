The Heterogeneous & Terminal Intelligence Lab is a research group in the School of Mathematical and Computer Sciences at Heriot-Watt University, Edinburgh. The lab is based in the National Robotarium, the UK's centre for robotics and artificial intelligence, where the principal investigator, Dr Chengjia Wang, is a Bicentennial Research Leader (Assistant Professor) and Programme Director of the BEng in Robotics.

## What we work on

Our research is organised around one question: how can learning systems cope with differences instead of assuming uniformity? Hospitals cannot always pool data, collaborating institutions rarely run identical infrastructure, and the devices that finally use a model — a scanner workstation, a surgical robot, an embedded sensor — operate under fixed resource budgets. We treat these constraints as part of the learning problem rather than as engineering afterthoughts.

This leads to four connected directions. **Heterogeneous intelligence** studies federated learning, knowledge distillation and cross-domain synthesis across institutions, architectures and hardware that differ from one another. **Trustworthy medical AI** develops segmentation, registration, image synthesis and quantitative phenotyping methods that are validated across scanners, protocols, centres and patient populations, including large-scale population imaging studies in UK Biobank. **Terminal intelligence** asks how capability can be transferred from large training environments to clinical and robotic endpoints through parameter-efficient adaptation, compression and data-efficient learning. **Heterogeneous robotic systems** extends the same ideas to perception, planning and coordination across physical agents with different sensing and actuation capabilities.

## How we work

The lab works in close collaboration with clinical and academic partners, including the University of Edinburgh's BHF Centre for Cardiovascular Science and Edinburgh Imaging, where the principal investigator holds an honorary fellowship. Much of our medical imaging work is conducted within governed data environments such as UK Biobank, and we aim to release code, preprints and project pages alongside our publications whenever agreements allow.

We also contribute to teaching and supervision within the School of Mathematical and Computer Sciences and the National Robotarium, training students in machine learning, medical image computing and robotics. Prospective doctoral researchers can find current routes to join the group on the [opportunities page](/opportunities/).
